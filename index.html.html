SPC · MSA · Capability
Statistical quality control — the decisions behind the methods
Four questions that separate engineers who run the tools from engineers who understand them.
Sample standard deviation captures all variation — including shifts and out-of-control events. R̄/d₂ estimates sigma from within-subgroup variation only, filtering out between-subgroup shifts. Using sample std dev inflates sigma, making the process look more capable than it is.
R̄/d₂ = process noise only · Sample std dev = everything, including problems
Cp measures whether your process spread fits the tolerance, assuming perfect centering. Cpk accounts for where the process is actually running. A great Cp with poor Cpk means you're producing off-center — potentially near a spec limit. In automotive, Cpk ≥ 1.33 is the minimum per AIAG.
Always report Cpk · Cp alone is incomplete and potentially misleading
Average & Range is an approximation. ANOVA decomposes variation into four sources: Part, Operator, Part×Operator interaction, and Repeatability. The interaction term captures whether operators measure differently depending on which part — Average & Range ignores this. AIAG MSA 4th Edition recommends ANOVA as the preferred method.
ANOVA reveals operator×part interaction · Average & Range hides it
Yes. Spec limits define what's acceptable. Control limits define what's statistically normal. Out of control inside spec means the part passes today — but something changed. Ignore it and the next subgroup may cross the spec limit. SPC catches drift before it makes bad parts.
Out of control = something changed · Act now, before it becomes out of spec
